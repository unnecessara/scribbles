So you want a job you can keep

Quit being a chronic quitter

April 25, 2019

Figuring out what to do for most of the hours in your day seems like an easy task. Find an opening, get a job, get paid. You can do that a few times and fail at the interview or quit shortly after taking the job because eventually, something you didn’t consider will come up, leaving you at the same starting point, only this time with a longer list of requirements or things to avoid. So you might as well start by clearly defining what you want in a job.

Just like job postings include a list of requirements they expect of you, so should you keep a collection of the things that you want in a job. Spend just as much time learning about the employer, as you spend learning about yourself.

It helps to separate concerns by categories:

The Subject

  • What problem is the company trying to solve? Can you get behind that?
  • If you don’t know a lot about the subject, do some research on it, figure out if it is something that catches your interest. Did you find yourself clicking on links to other articles about related matters? Can you relate your own life experiences to what you read? Did you afterward daydream about other solutions within that topic, or how the company could improve or do things differently?
  • If you were to get the job and someone who knows you well were to ask you where you work, could you explain it, and say it in a genuinely positive tone?

The Actual Operations

  • Don’t skim through the ‘what you will be doing’ part of the job posting. Take it seriously. They will often include statements with lists of items, for instance ‘Views OOP, MVC, and version control as essential to building good software’. Don’t be okay with knowing 80% of each statement. If you are less than 99% sure about one of the items, go research it, understand how to use it, be able to define it in your own words, out loud. Yes, talk to yourself out loud.
  • If you expect to write and send your application the same day you are missing the point. Preparation requires time, be patient. Go write a throwaway piece of code for every few points in the list of ‘what you will be doing’. For instance, you may think, 'I work on my own, and haven’t had much need for version control, but I know how it works'. That's not good enough. Start a repository for a practice project, commit your changes, create feature branches, try to get yourself in a git pickle. Resolve your problems, resolve yourself.
  • Every day that you are working towards the application, read a few articles before starting. It will expose you to different ideas, it will help expand your vocabulary and improve your writing skills. As a bonus, you will be ‘staying up-to-date on best practices, and new tech’, a requirement almost every job posting includes.
    Side tip: set a time limit to this exercise as it can become a great procrastinating activity.

Interpersonal Stuff

  • Don’t be self-deceptive. Go with the hard facts and evidence, instead of imagining what you could, or would like to be like. Take a walk on the retrospective side. What in the past have you been successful at when it comes to working with people? What have you not enjoyed or has soured things when working with people? Make two lists that complete the sentences: I liked when ____ and I didn’t like when ____. Be analytical and draw conclusions from those. For instance, I could have the following items:
    1. I didn’t like when my boss would stand behind me and I had to explain on the spot what I was doing.
    2. I did not like not knowing who I could ask for help.
    3. I did like having scheduled weekly check-ups on progress on specific tasks.
    From these, I could conclude that I like having a structure where it is clear when and to whom I have to report progress, and who I can ask for help. Recognizing these things will help you describe your expectations during interviews, which is an important part of finding the right job for you.

Learning and Styles

  • In a job interview, you should be prepared to answer a lot of questions, but you should also come prepared with questions for your employer. Don’t forget that this is how you determine whether you will be able to be happy or satisfied for most of the hours of most of the days in the week. Make sure that some of those questions include asking the employer how she supports continuous learning because let’s face it, programming is a life-long learning journey.
  • Find out what is your learning style or styles. If you don’t know what that means, read something about it. Or play this fun game that helps with memory. Try to guess what it means, write it down, then look it up and compare answers and see how far from the truth you were.
  • A lot of companies do (if not, run away, they should) set aside time, money and or resources for learning. These can be in the form of time during work hours to further your learning by working on your own stuff, paid conferences, access to online classes and tutorials, or connections with groups in the community to do volunteering work, meetups, Ted talks, etc.
  • A very important question to ask is how the company’s onboarding process works. I can’t think of a worse experience than being thrown into a project with no introduction, and with expectations of producing at the same rate as people who have worked there for years. It is important that the employer understands there has to be a period at the beginning where you will be going at slug-pace, while you are understanding the inner workings of the project. During this time you should have the necessary resources and support to guide you. Ask about it.
  • Remember, continuous learning will keep you motivated, engaged, and dynamic, as opposed to bored, apathetic and narrow-minded.

Schedules

  • Another area of potential self-deception. Smarten up! Wake up that brutal realism in you.
  • What are parts of your day to day that are important to you? What can’t you live without? Don’t undervalue them, however trivial they may sound. For me that could be, walking the dog two or three times, exercising, cooking dinner.
  • Now think of the things you want to accomplish for yourself outside of work. If you don’t have any, read this. Now try to see when you could find time to do those things. Night classes, weekend sessions, early mornings. You might think mostly in money terms, but time is another form of currency, and similarly, it’s all about budgeting.
  • When looking at job openings, find out what their time expectations are. Is it a 9-5 strict schedule? Could you come into work earlier, or later, or maybe work more hours fewer days? Will you have to work overtime? What are the overtime policies, justified overtime when you have to finish work before a deadline is never as bad as obligatory overtime when you’re just sitting waiting for work to be handed to you.
  • What is their vacation policy? Will you have enough time to do the things you like doing, visiting family, traveling the world, building a house, dream big, it’s important.
  • An important element, often overlooked, is the commute time. If it takes you an hour to get there and an hour to return that is an extra two hours less that you have to work on your side projects, exercise and or life things. But consider making the commute one of those things, and it might just make sense. If you can and like to, you could bike, skate or run to work, bam! That’s your daily exercise. If your office is pet-friendly, you could walk the dog to work and back, there’s your life thing. If you ride a bus that allows you to work on your writing, reading, knitting, etc. Then there’s your side project time. So aside from budgeting, time management is also about smart organizing.

The Pay

  • Are you a maximizer, or a don’t care as long as it gets me by kind of person? Either way, this is a good time to realistically assess your financial needs.
  • Look up average pay for the position you are going for. Use different sources to get a better idea. Compare that against the salary the employer offers.
  • Do the dreaded look back through your bank statement, just do it, like pulling off a bandaid. Use a smart app like mint if you want. Get an idea of how much money you spend monthly. Compare that against the salary the employer offers.
  • Make sure you consider how much you will be able to save after expenses. If you don’t think you should be saving. I think you might be wrong. But I don’t know much about saving, so let’s all read more about what we don’t know okay?
  • I included this section last so you wouldn’t think about it until the end. It’s not that it isn’t important, it is just that it's often the only consideration. Remember, there are other currencies, other ways to feel valued and other ways to track productivity.

Getting a job sometimes feels like a full-time job. But hey, if it helps, it’ll get you trained for working long hours, getting organized and becoming a better problem solver. Not to mention gaining valuable knowledge of (your)self, great song btw. Don’t waste your time applying to jobs you will not be content with. Consider all angles, this will be your life for the next who knows how many years. Don’t be lazy about it, be selective and deliberate.

When you pick the jobs you want and apply to them, don’t go to interviews feeling under-practiced. Think back to those exams you showed up unprepared for, it is the worst feeling. Prepare enough so that you are not only comfortable talking about everything on the job posting but also intrigued to see how the employer will respond to your questions.
